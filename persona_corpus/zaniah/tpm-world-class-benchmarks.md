# TPM and world-class benchmarks (strategic reference)

*Paraphrased reference notes (license-clean). TPM as defined by JIPM; OEE per the
Nakajima framework; benchmarks per SMRP and industry practice.*

## Total Productive Maintenance (TPM)

TPM is a culture-and-system approach that makes equipment effectiveness everyone's job,
especially operators. It rests on eight pillars on a foundation of 5S:

1. **Autonomous maintenance** — operators clean, inspect, lubricate, and catch early
   defects ("operators own the cleanliness, technicians own the complexity").
2. **Planned maintenance** — scheduled, data-driven PM by the maintenance team.
3. **Focused improvement (Kaizen)** — small cross-functional teams eliminate chronic losses.
4. **Quality maintenance** — design out quality defects caused by equipment condition.
5. **Early equipment management** — feed reliability lessons into the design/commissioning
   of new equipment.
6. **Education and training** — build skills across operators and technicians.
7. **Safety, health, environment** — zero accidents as a precondition, not a trade-off.
8. **TPM in administration** — extend the loss-elimination mindset to office processes.

TPM attacks the **six big losses**: breakdowns, setup/adjustment, idling/minor stops,
reduced speed, startup/yield, and quality defects/rework — which map directly onto OEE.

## OEE and the six losses

**OEE = Availability × Performance × Quality.**

- **Availability** loses to breakdowns and setup (the downtime losses).
- **Performance** loses to minor stops and reduced speed (the speed losses).
- **Quality** loses to defects, rework, and startup yield (the quality losses).

Because OEE multiplies three fractions, a "good-looking" 90% on each axis is only
0.9³ ≈ **73% OEE** — small losses compound. Always decompose OEE into its three factors
to find which loss to attack first; a single headline OEE number hides the lever.

## World-class benchmarks (use as direction, not destiny)

- **OEE** — world-class discrete manufacturing is often cited around **85%**
  (≈ 90% availability × 95% performance × 99.9% quality). Many plants start near 40–60%;
  the gap is the opportunity.
- **Reliability** — track **MTBF** trending up and **MTTR** trending down over time;
  the trend matters more than any single absolute number, which is industry-specific.
- **PM program health (SMRP-style)** — PM compliance ≥ ~90%; planned/scheduled work a
  large majority of total work (world-class proactive shops run ~80%+ planned, with
  reactive/emergency work a small minority); schedule compliance high; low rework.

## The strategist's use

Benchmarks set direction, not a number to fake. Measure your real OEE, decompose it,
pick the biggest of the six losses, and sequence improvement against it. World-class is
the outcome of climbing the maturity ladder rung by rung — not a switch you flip.
